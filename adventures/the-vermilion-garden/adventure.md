# One-Shot: The Vermilion Garden

A single-session **murder mystery** for **five level-3 characters**. Play time: **~4–5 hours**. Bring your own party — no pre-mades.

This is the social-and-investigation counterpart to the combat one-shots in this folder ([The Pale Gate](../the-pale-gate/adventure.md), [The Bell of Ashvane](../the-bell-of-ashvane/adventure.md)). There are only **two fights in the whole adventure**, both at the climax. The body of the session is **talking to people, reading a crowd, following evidence, and not getting the wrong person killed.** It is built to make a **Charisma character the star** — the party that can persuade, charm, intimidate, perform, and hold its nerve will crack this where a party of silent murderhobos will flail.

It takes its bones from *Blood on the Ice* (Skyrim): a beloved local is the killer, a feared outsider is the scapegoat the crowd wants to lynch, and **the clock keeps killing people until you stop it.** The misdirection is the point. Read **The truth (GM only)** and **Running a mystery (read this)** before you run it.

## The pitch

**Aurivane** is the great warm river-city of the south — terraced and golden, a hundred thousand souls, and right now in the breathless heart of the **Midsummer Bloomfeast**, the festival that turns the whole city into a riot of flowers, music, and wine for a week. And nowhere blooms harder than **the Verdance**: Aurivane's famous garden-quarter, a district of public rose-gardens, perfumeries, tea-terraces, theaters, and flower-markets where the rich come to play and the whole city comes to fall in love. Its crown is the **Vermilion Garden** — a terraced hill of impossible roses, the most beautiful place in the south.

And three people are dead in three weeks. Each found among flowers at night — a beloved actress in the tea-gardens, a sweet old confectioner by the market fountain, the festival's own crowned Garland-Prince at the foot of the Vermilion Garden. Each **drained near-white of blood through a single fine cut**, each drenched in a gorgeous unplaceable perfume, each missing one treasured keepsake, and each left with **a single cut vermilion rose laid on the breast.** The Verdance is whispering about the **Briar Bride** — the old midsummer ghost-story of the maiden who takes the most beautiful and leaves a flower in their place.

The festival can't stop — there's too much money and too much pride in it — but the district is fraying: doors barred behind garlands, a mob forming around a feared old poison-gardener, and the city Watch stretched paper-thin by a million revelers. A magistrate of Aurivane hires the party, quietly, as **investigators with a Watch writ**: find the killer and end it before the Verdance lynches the wrong neighbor or the Bloomfeast ends in blood. **200 gold now, 200 on a name and a body or a confession.**

There are **three nights until Midsummer.** That is not a coincidence. The killer knows it isn't.

## The truth (GM only)

The murderer is **Florian Sael, the master perfumer and garden-keeper of the Verdance** — the gentle, generous artisan who tends the Vermilion Garden itself, breeds its famous roses, and bottles the scent of midsummer for half the city. He is the most-loved man in the district: he gives flowers to children and attar to brides, hosts the festival's crowning, and has been *helping the Watch* with the investigation. **No one suspects him. That is his whole defense, and it is a good one.**

Years ago, Florian's daughter **Mira** died of summer-fever on Midsummer night — eight years old, the joy of his life — and he has spent every summer since trying to grow her back. In his locked private conservatory, rooted in consecrated earth over what's left of her, he has cultivated the **Daughter-Rose**: a great vermilion briar grown into the shape of a girl. He has come to believe, with absolute sincerity, that he can call her *back into bloom* — that a soul is "warmth," and warmth lives in two things: **blood**, and the **one object a person loved most** (which holds a little of who they were). So he hunts the **most radiant, beloved, joyful people in the Verdance** — the brightest "essences" — drains their blood to feed the briar's roots, takes the keepsake that holds their warmth and weaves it into the branches, and leaves a perfect vermilion rose in its place. *A fair trade, in his mind. He is not a monster to himself. He is a grieving father gathering the sweetest of the city to make his daughter a body of its best.*

He invented nothing of the "Briar Bride" — that legend was here before him — but he **feeds it**, because a ghost can't be arrested. He does his killing on his nightly rounds gathering blooms (the oils are richest at dawn and dusk), which is the one reason a man can walk the dark gardens with a knife and a basket and have no one think twice.

On **Midsummer night** (night three), the anniversary of Mira's death, he intends to make her bloom. He needs **one more warmth** — and if the party stalls, he takes it from someone they've met. If they let the ritual finish, the thing in the conservatory **opens its eyes**, and it is not Mira, and it is not kind.

**The two scapegoats** (he's content to let either hang):
- **Dama Vesh**, the poison-gardener of the Witherrows — feared as a hedge-witch, abrasive, and circumstantially *drenched* in evidence. The district already calls her the Briar Bride made flesh. The mob wants her. **She is innocent.**
- **Grenn**, the public-gardens' groundskeeper who finds the bodies at dawn — surly, strong, bloody-handed, always first to the corpses. **Innocent** (but a petty thief, which makes him act guilty).

**The decoys — because everyone here is charming, and everyone has a secret.** The Verdance runs on charm; half the district is warm, generous, and *hiding something*, so Florian's kindness is camouflage, not a red flag. A genre-savvy party that "instantly clocks the suspiciously nice guy" will clock the **wrong** one. Three charming locals are built to soak up that suspicion, eat the party's nights, and feel like the answer right up until the next body proves they weren't:
- **Castellan Vane**, the theatrical impresario who *adores* the macabre — the obvious "charming villain," and a deliberate decoy. He even bought some of Mira Sael's stolen grave-goods, so the *motive trail runs through him.*
- **Madame Ysolde**, the beloved festival patroness — who had a real, specific motive to silence the first victim.
- **Deputy Roon**, the helpful Watch officer — corrupt, and actively burying evidence.
None of them is the killer. But the loud decoys only handle the *cynical* instinct ("follow the secrets"). The **genre-savvy** instinct — *"it's always the kind one with the dead loved one and the locked room"* — is blunted a different way: the Verdance is **full of gentle, grieving artisans who share Florian's very traits.** A beekeeper out among the blooms at dawn; a dollmaker lovingly recreating the dead behind a locked door; a whole cohort of parents who lost children to the same fever and weep every Midsummer. **Being the kind soul with a dead child and a private workshop is not a clue here — it's a type**, and Florian is one face in it. (Full details in **The cast** — both *The charming and the shady* and *The gentle ones*.) See **The intended arc**, below — most parties chase a suspect and *lose someone* before the truth surfaces. That's by design.

The mystery is solvable. The clues point at Florian for anyone who follows the **rose**, the **wound**, and the **pattern** — but every one of those clues *also* has a reading that points at Dama Vesh, and Florian's good name papers over the rest. The party's job is to see through a man everybody loves.

## Why this favors Charisma (how to run it)

The whole adventure is tuned so that **presence, not perception, is the master key.** Per [../../rules/04-skills-and-checks.md](../../rules/04-skills-and-checks.md), social rolls key off **Charisma**; reading whether someone is *lying* is Mind/Insight. Build the session so the Charisma player is doing things constantly and the high-Mind player is their indispensable partner:

- **People are frightened and tight-lipped** (and, mid-festival, suspicious of out-of-town investigators sniffing around their good time). Most won't volunteer what they know. **Charisma (Persuasion) opens them; Charisma (Intimidation) cracks the stubborn ones; Performance / an honest warm manner gets the Witherrows poor to trust outsiders.** A blunt, charmless party gets shrugs and has to work three times as hard.
- **Access is gated socially, not by locks.** The body, the Watch's notes, the locked conservatory, the temple records — the *intended* route to each is **talk your way in** (persuade the sergeant, charm Florian into a tour, perform the writ with enough authority). Lockpicking and breaking-and-entering work too, with consequences; the social route is the rewarded one.
- **The mob is a Charisma boss fight with no HP bar.** Talking down (or steering) a frightened festival crowd is the adventure's central set-piece, resolved entirely on Charisma. (Scene 4.)
- **The killer is an innate (Charisma) caster.** Florian charms with scent and word. **Charisma defense** is what keeps a PC in the fight, and the *cleanest* ending is a **Charisma showdown** — getting him to confess in front of witnesses, or talking him off the ledge — not a stabbing.

**Spotlight the high-Charisma PC, but never let charm be a "win button" that skips the work.** Charisma gets people *talking*; the *content* still has to be reasoned out (that's the Mind player). And a great Persuasion roll never makes a true thing false: you can charm Dama Vesh into helping you, but you cannot charm her into being the killer she isn't. See **Running a mystery**, below.

## The shape of the session

The middle is open — players investigate in whatever order they like. These are the **beats**, not a rail:

| Beat | Type | Threat | Where |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Arrival + fresh crime scene** | — | The Vermilion Garden |
| 2 | **District investigation** (witnesses, the legend, the bodies) | — | The Verdance |
| 3 | **The suspects** (interrogations + misdirection) | — | Around the district |
| 4 | **The mob rises** (social set-piece / skill challenge) | ~ (see Scene 4) | The Witherrows |
| 5 | **The perfumery & the conservatory** | **6** (combat, optional route) | Sael's Perfumery |
| 5b | *If the party stalls* — **the fourth murder** | — | (a named NPC dies) |
| 6 | **The Midsummer confrontation** (climax) | **16 early / ~26 if too slow** | The conservatory |

Budget reference: five level-3 PCs have an encounter budget of **15 threat** (party level × players; [../../rules/13-encounters-and-rewards.md](../../rules/13-encounters-and-rewards.md)). The climax runs **~107% if they catch him before Midsummer** and **~170% (a real boss) if they let the ritual complete** — the slow party gets the harder fight. That's the teeth of the clock.

## The clock — three nights to Midsummer

Two clocks run at once. **Surface both to the players; let the pressure drive their choices** (the co-GM's job is to track them — [../../rules/16-co-gm-guide.md](../../rules/16-co-gm-guide.md)).

**Clock A — the killer.** Florian needs one last "warmth," and he takes it on **Midsummer night (night three)** unless stopped. But he is *watching the investigation* (he keeps offering to help). **If, by the end of any night, the party has openly accused or arrested the wrong person — or simply has no idea it's him — he is emboldened and kills again that same night.** A fourth body resets the district's fear *and* hands the party a fresh, sloppier crime scene (see Scene 5b). Make the fourth victim **someone they've met and liked** — the tavern-keeper's daughter, a witness who helped them. The cost of being slow has a face.

**Clock B — the mob.** Fear curdles into violence, faster because the festival has the whole district drunk and raw. Track it as a **mood in three steps**, advancing each in-game evening and each time the party publicly fumbles (accuses someone and is wrong, lets a scare spread, fails badly with the crowd):
1. *Mutters.* Garlands over barred doors, the Witherrows shunned, Dama Vesh's gate daubed with warding-paint.
2. *Torches.* A knot of frightened revelers gathers, half-drunk on festival wine and fear, "going to ask the poison-woman some questions."
3. *The lynching.* The mob marches on Dama Vesh's cottage. **If the party doesn't defuse it (Scene 4), they kill her** — and the party loses their best living witness *and* gains a district that now thinks the killing is solved while Florian walks free to bloom on Midsummer.

The clocks interact: the **fastest clean win** is to crack the case before night three *and* keep the mob off Dama Vesh long enough to use what she knows. **Never let a clock hard-stall the plot** — they spend *time* and cost *lives*, they never delete the next clue (see below).

## The intended arc — expect to lose someone (read this)

This mystery is *hard on purpose*, and it is built so that **most parties — call it 7 in 10 — will lose an NPC before they crack it.** That is not a failure state; it's the emotional spine, the same one *Blood on the Ice* runs: you suspect the wrong person, you act on it, and the cost of being wrong is a body. Lean into it rather than rescuing them from it:

- **The middle of the adventure pulls hard toward the decoys** — Castellan Vane especially. A clever party will assemble a strong, *wrong* case and feel good about it. Let them.
- **Acting on the wrong suspect advances Clock A.** Arrest Castellan, corner Ysolde, jail Grenn — and that night Florian kills again (Scene 5b), or the mob takes Dama Vesh (Scene 4). The party learns they were wrong the worst possible way.
- **The loss *is* the breakthrough.** Design the Night-2 death to teach: the new body is killed in a way that *exonerates whoever the party just accused* and points at the rose / the dawn flower-cart / the commissioned grave. From there the real solve is fast. The guilt of the loss is the engine that finally focuses them.
- **The fast 30%** — parties who dig the *motive* early (the empty grave → Skein → "the flower-man"), or tail Florian on his dawn rounds, or follow the unique rose to its only grower — can crack it by the end of Night 1 or Day 2 and *prevent* the second killing. That's a genuinely great, rare run. Reward it lavishly.

**Don't fudge to spare them, and don't fudge to punish them.** Run the clocks straight and let the loss fall out of the fiction: most parties chase the charming ghoul, and a real person dies for it. *That* is the adventure.

## Three days, three nights — the timeline

The Bloomfeast runs day and night, and **what the party can do changes with the clock.** Track it; let it squeeze.

- **Days** — the district is awake and the festival roars. Time for the **open game**: talking, charming, bribing, attending parties, questioning the market, viewing the bodies. The temple records are open, the suspects are out and accessible. Loud, crowded, safe.
- **Nights** — the festival narrows to lantern-lit revelry and the quiet corners go dark. Time for the **hidden game**: shops, the conservatory, and the Watch-house are locked (break-and-enter), suspects can be *tailed* on their night business, the mob forms — and **the killer hunts.** Being out at night is how you catch him; it's also how you become a victim or a witness. Theft, deception, and breaking-in live here.

A typical run:

| When | What's live |
|---|---|
| **Day 1** (arrive) | The fresh scene at the garden; meet Halloran, Florian, the district. Suspicion not yet formed. |
| **Night 1** | First festival night. Eavesdrop the masque, tail a suspect, break into a shop. The killer is *gathering*, not yet killing — a near-miss, a glimpsed flower-cart. The free night to set up. |
| **Day 2** | The case deepens; the decoys (Castellan, Ysolde, Roon) look guiltier; mob mutters rise. Most parties build their *wrong* case today. |
| **Night 2** | **The pivot.** Unless the party already has the truth, Florian kills a beloved NPC they've met (Scene 5b) — or the mob lynches Dama Vesh (Scene 4). The loss. The breakthrough clue arrives with the body. |
| **Day 3** | Armed with the breakthrough, the decoy is cleared and the net closes on Florian. The race to beat Midsummer. |
| **Night 3 — Midsummer** | The climax (Scene 6). Confront him before the ritual, or arrive as the Bloomwake opens its eyes. |

If the party tries to "rest and pick it up tomorrow," remind them — through an NPC, or just the rising moon — that **the nights are when people die.** The clock is not a backdrop; it is the killer's schedule.

## Running a mystery (read this)

A murder mystery dies two ways: the players get **stuck** (no clue to follow) or they get **railroaded** (one "correct" deduction gated behind one roll). This adventure is built to avoid both. The rules already tell you how — [../../rules/01-core-mechanic.md](../../rules/01-core-mechanic.md) ("reading a clue the GM wrote into the world needs no roll") and [../../rules/16-co-gm-guide.md](../../rules/16-co-gm-guide.md) ("never gate on one roll; fail forward"). Three working rules for *this* session:

1. **The three-clue rule.** Every conclusion the party must reach has **at least three** independent clues pointing at it (see the **Evidence** section). They only need to find one. If they miss two, the third is waiting. **No single roll, room, or NPC is load-bearing.**
2. **Rolls add color and access, not permission to think.** If a PC looks where a clue is, they *find* it — no Perception/Investigation roll. Rolls are for the *deeper read* (what made this wound? whose rose is this?) and for **social access** (will this witness talk? will the sergeant hand over the body?). A failed roll never deletes a clue; it costs **time** (advance a clock) or gives the **gist instead of the gold** (tiered table below).
3. **Charm reveals, it doesn't rewrite.** A brilliant Persuasion roll makes an NPC *tell the truth they know* — it cannot make a false suspect guilty or a guilty man innocent. Dama Vesh charmed to her bones still didn't do it. Florian charmed into a confession is the *win*, but he only slips when the party has the **Daughter-Rose** or enough cornering evidence — charm alone won't make an innocent-acting man invent a crime he'd never admit. **The dice open mouths; the truth is fixed.**

**Tiered outcomes** — decide the bands before each notable roll ([../../rules/16-co-gm-guide.md](../../rules/16-co-gm-guide.md)):

| Result | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **< 10** | They clam up / you get nothing this approach — and it *costs* (a clock ticks, or you've made an enemy). |
| **10–14** | The gist. The shape of the answer, not the detail. ("Aye, Lysenne had a sweetheart, some token — couldn't tell you what.") |
| **15–19** | The real answer, clean. |
| **20+ / nat 20** | The answer *plus* a thread they didn't ask for (a new lead, a name, a witness who saw more). |

## The Verdance — the district

A terraced garden-quarter of a great warm city, drunk on midsummer. Loud, fragrant, crowded, and — in its quiet corners at night — frightened. Key locations:

- **The Vermilion Garden** — the terraced public rose-garden on the district's high hill, the Verdance's glory and Aurivane's pride. Florian breeds and tends it. The third (freshest) body was found at its foot. From its top terrace you can see every murder site in the district laid out below — *which means the killer could too.*
- **The Bloommarket** — the great flower-and-honey market square, the social heart of the Verdance, packed for the festival. Body two was found by its fountain. The best place to *hear the district.*
- **The Tea-Gardens** — terraced pleasure-gardens of tea-houses, theaters, and pavilions where the rich play. Body one was found here. Home turf of the actress-victim's company.
- **The Witherrows** — the run-down, overgrown low corner of the district where the poor and the strange live, and where **Dama Vesh** keeps her walled poison-garden. The festival doesn't reach here. The district shuns it; the mob will march on it.
- **The Temple of the Summer Mother** (a warmth-and-harvest faith) — keeps the district's birth-and-death records. **Mira Sael's death is recorded here.** Tended by **Brother Anselm**, kindly and overwhelmed by festival rites.
- **The Watch-house** — **Sergeant Halloran**'s seat and the district cells. He's drowning under the festival, wants an easy answer, and is leaning toward arresting Dama Vesh to calm the crowd.
- **The Gilded Finch** — the district's grand festival tavern, all music and wine and gossip; the mob forms on its steps.
- **Sael's Perfumery** — Florian's beautiful shop just off the Bloommarket: a warm, gorgeous front of glass bottles and hanging blooms, a distillery in back, and a **locked private conservatory** "where the rare grafts can't be disturbed." The Daughter-Rose is in there. This is the *Hjerim* of the adventure — the place with the truth in it.

## The cast

*Aurivane is a great cosmopolitan river-port; its people are of every ancestry. Each NPC's race is tagged in* **(bold)** *— flavor only, nothing else about them changes.*

### The victims (all real, all radiant)
- **Lysenne** **(Satyr)** (3 weeks ago, the Tea-Gardens) — the toast of the Verdance theater, an actress and singer everyone half-loved. **Taken: a pearl earring, her late mother's.** Bright, generous, the warmest voice in the district. (A performer — Charisma made flesh. Florian took the sweetest voice in town.)
- **Old Pateros** **(Stout Halfling)** (8 days ago, the Bloommarket fountain) — the honey-and-candied-flower confectioner, everyone's grandfather. **Taken: a locket of his late wife.** Gentle, beloved, gave sweets to the market children.
- **Castan** **(Eladrin)** (3 nights ago, the foot of the Vermilion Garden) — the festival's crowned **Garland-Prince**, a charming, popular youth chosen to lead the Bloomfeast. **Taken: the silver garland-pin of his crowning.** Golden, joyful, adored — and his death is what tipped the district into panic.

> **The pattern (GM):** every victim was *radiant, beloved, joyful*, each spoke warmly with Florian within days of dying (he selects them, gives them blooms, asks what they love), each drained through one clean fine cut, each drenched in a beautiful unplaceable perfume, each left with a single perfect vermilion rose on the breast where a treasured keepsake was taken.

### Florian Sael — the perfumer & garden-keeper *(the killer)* · **Half-Elf**
*Want:* to make Mira bloom again before Midsummer. To be loved and trusted (he genuinely is, and needs to stay so).
*Manner:* gentle, funny, self-deprecating, endlessly generous. Presses a free vial of attar on the party, asks after their families, *offers to help investigate* and means it — it's perfect cover and he half-believes he's innocent.
*Tell (don't flag it):* he asks unusually tender, specific questions about the dead — *what did they love? what did they treasure most?* — and about the party. The conservatory is always locked "for the rare grafts." He goes very still and changes the subject if **Mira** is named. He never has an alibi for the murder nights because he was *out gathering blooms before dawn, alone* — and no one questions that.
*Under pressure:* warm and reasonable, never rattled — until the party produces the **Daughter-Rose** or boxes him in with the **rose + pattern + Mira** together. Then the gentleness cracks into a terrible, tearful certainty that he's been *kind.* See Scene 6 and his stat block.

### The gentle ones *(Florian's true camouflage — all innocent)*

This is the tier that fixes the real problem: **you must not be able to find the killer by asking "who's the suspiciously nice, grieving craftsman?"** — because in the Verdance that is a *type*, not a tell. Where the loud decoys (Castellan, Ysolde, Roon) soak up the cynical instinct, these quiet, genuinely lovely people soak up the genre-savvy one. **Run them with exactly the warmth, grief, and helpfulness you give Florian.** None is the killer; each shares one or more of his "tells."

**Edran — the gardens' beekeeper *(Firbolg)*.** A gentle, beloved old man who keeps the hives among the flowering terraces and presses honeycomb on every child in the market. He lost his own boy to the **summer-fever** years ago and still tends a small grave. *Why he reads guilty:* he is **out among the gardens at dawn and dusk** — the very hours and places of the killings — for the wholly innocent reason that bees and blooms keep that schedule, and he carries a **thin curved hive-knife.** He is the *honest* version of the cover Florian abuses: a party hunting "who's in the gardens before dawn?" finds **two** gentle craftsmen there, not one. Innocent, and quietly wounded to be asked.

**Lensa — the memorial dollmaker *(Forest Gnome)*.** A soft-spoken, adored artisan. In the Verdance it's an old, tender custom to commission a doll of a lost loved one, and Lensa is the finest hand for it — so the grieving come to her, and she **asks them gentle, searching questions about the dead** (*"what did she love? how did she laugh?"*) to make each doll *true*, and keeps the work in a **locked studio** until it's done. *Why she reads guilty:* she is, right now, making memorial dolls of **all three victims** — their families commissioned them — so she has likenesses of the dead, intimate notes on what each one treasured, and a locked room full of them, having sought out exactly the details a killer would want. She is a near-perfect mirror of Florian's crime — *recreating the dead through loving craft* — done with nothing but tenderness. A party that breaks in expecting a charnel-house finds a shrine of grief and an innocent woman who'll never feel clean about how they looked at her. **This is the sharpest piece of misdirection in the adventure; spend it well.**

**The fever-bereaved.** Plant early and often that the **summer-fever, eight years ago, killed a whole cohort of the district's children on one terrible Midsummer** — and that *many* beloved Verdance figures lost someone then and grieve every festival. A widow, **Marise**, tends the public memorial rose-bed (planted, as it happens, by Florian himself); others light candles; the temple holds a remembrance the eve of Midsummer. So *"lost a child to the fever, grieves on Midsummer"* fits a dozen sympathetic people — it does **not** finger one perfumer. Florian's motive only damns him once the party *also* has the rose, the cut, the cart, or the commissioned body to cut him out from every other grieving parent in the quarter.

> **GM — the whole point of this tier:** spread Florian's tells across these NPCs so that *kindness, grief, a dead child, odd hours in the gardens, tender questions about the victims, a locked private room* each belong to **several** people. The killer is found by **evidence** — the unique graft, the carried-off blood, the dawn flower-cart, Skein's testimony — and never by vibe. A table that "just knows it's the nice flower guy" is, on what they can actually see, no more justified than knowing it's the nice bee man or the nice doll lady. **Make them prove it.**

### Dama Vesh — the poison-gardener *(scapegoat A — innocent)* · **Abyssal Tiefling**
*Want:* to be left alone; to not be blamed again (a child she couldn't save once died of her remedy, and the district never forgot). To protect the Witherrows folk who rely on her medicines.
*Manner:* abrasive, proud, sharp-tongued, **will not explain herself to people who already think she's a witch** — which makes her look guilty as sin. Warms *only* to a party that treats her with genuine respect (Charisma, and the *right approach* — humility, not threats).
*Why she looks guilty (red herrings, all explainable):*
- **Blood-stained shears and rags** in her cottage — she grafts roses and doctors animals and people; she bleeds fevers.
- **A bed of deep-red roses** in her walled garden — common briar-stock, *not* Florian's cultivar (a clue *through* the red herring: a sharp eye or Dama herself can tell the two apart).
- **Seen near Pateros's body** at dawn — she found him, tried to close his eyes; the market-folk saw her over the corpse and assumed the worst.
- She **knows the Briar Bride legend** cold and recites it with relish — because she's the only one who remembers the real midsummer drowning-of-a-bride it came from. (And she remembers **Mira Sael.**)
*What she gives a party that earns her:* the **rose distinction** (the bloom on the bodies is Florian's rare "Sael's Vermilion," not garden-common stock — *"that's his graft, no one else in the south grows it"* → Conclusion 1); the **wound read** (she's seen all three bodies — *"that's a grafting-knife's edge, fine and curved, not a footpad's blade nor any beast"* → Conclusion 2); and — the big one — she **remembers Mira Sael dying on Midsummer, and that Florian "never buried his grief, just kept growing roses where she's planted."**

### Grenn — the groundskeeper *(scapegoat B — innocent)* · **Half-Orc**
*Want:* to not get caught for the small thing he *did* do — **he lifted a purse and a ring off Castan's body** before raising the alarm. That's why he's shifty, lies about his movements, and sweats when questioned.
*Why he looks guilty:* surly, huge, dirt and blood under his nails (he prunes the gardens and hauls the corpses out of the beds), always first to the bodies (dawn rounds), was overheard saying something ugly about the Garland-Prince ("prancing little peacock"). *Means and opportunity by the bucket.*
*The break:* press him (Intimidation cracks him; Persuasion that "we're hunting a murderer, not a purse-snatch" opens him) and he confesses to **robbing Castan's corpse** — not killing — and gives up that, the dawn he found Castan, he saw **Florian's flower-cart already up among the high terraces and smelled his attar on the breeze, when not another soul was about.** He didn't think anything of it. *The flower-man's always up before the birds.* (→ Conclusion 4, opportunity.)

### The charming and the shady *(decoys — innocent of murder, guilty of plenty)*

In the Verdance, *warm and helpful is the default setting.* These three exist so the party can't solve the case by smelling out "the suspiciously nice one" — there are four nice ones, and the nicest-seeming is a decoy. Each is a satisfying suspect, each has a genuine ugly secret, and **none of them is the killer.**

**Castellan Vane — impresario of the Verdance theater *(Drow)* (the decoy villain).**
*Manner:* magnetic, theatrical, delighted by death; hosts masked midnight revels; wrote the season's hit play, *The Briar Bride's Kiss.* The single most *fun to suspect* person in the city, and he enjoys the role.
*Why he looks guilty:* obsessed with the macabre; keeps a locked private gallery of funerary curios and "relics"; the victims (the actress, the Garland-Prince) all orbited his theater; out at all hours; openly savors the killings as "exquisite, simply operatic."
*His secret (real, not murder):* he is a **fence for grave-robbed funerary art**, and — the hook — **he bought some of Mira Sael's grave-goods** years ago from a resurrection-man. So the trail *"who disturbed the dead?"* runs straight through his gallery. He's a ghoul and a criminal. He is not the killer.
*Role:* the party's prime suspect through the middle of the adventure. Busting him — B&E the gallery, steal his ledger, bribe his stagehands — feels exactly like solving it. It is the moment *before* the loss. When the next body drops with Castellan in a cell, the party learns the shape of their mistake.

**Madame Ysolde — festival patroness *(High Elf)*.**
*Manner:* warm, witty, endlessly generous; funds the theater and half the Bloomfeast; everyone's favorite hostess.
*Why she looks guilty:* she and the first victim, **Lysenne**, fell out viciously weeks before the murder; she has money, reach, and a hundred people who owe her; she's slippery about where she was that night.
*Her secret (real, not murder):* she's drowning in debt and quietly selling a forbidden euphoric **"bliss-attar"** under festival cover — and **Lysenne found out and threatened to expose her.** A real motive, for *one* victim. But she was trying to *buy* Lysenne's silence, not cut her throat, and she has no link to Pateros or Castan. Innocent of murder; guilty of dealing, and of lying about all of it.
*Role:* a strong, narrow red herring for Victim 1. A tangle of bribery, deception, and Insight — and a dead end that costs a night.

**Deputy Roon — the helpful face of the Watch *(Hobgoblin)*.**
*Manner:* easy-going, accommodating, *"anything you need, friends"* — the cooperative officer who'll fetch you the file and walk you to the morgue.
*Why he misleads:* he runs a quiet **protection racket** on festival vendors, and he **buried a witness statement** — a flower-girl who saw the flower-cart on the high terrace before dawn — because chasing it would have exposed where *he* was that night (shaking down a stall two streets over). His "help" is curated to protect himself, so it subtly steers the party toward Dama Vesh.
*Role:* the bribe-broker (he'll sell the case file, morgue access, a blind eye to move at night) whose information is *tainted.* The buried statement is a clean Conclusion-4 clue he's sitting on — it surfaces only if the party leans on him hard, catches him in the racket, or breaks into the Watch-house evidence locker.

### Skein — the resurrection-man *(Witherrows — the linchpin)* · **Goblin**

A grave-robber who'll talk for coin or under threat, holed up in the Witherrows. He is the knot that ties the trails together: years ago he was paid by **"the flower-man"** to quietly dig a child's body out of the temple ground and deliver it — and he sold the *grave-goods* on to **Castellan.** Find Skein (via the empty grave, via Castellan's ledger, or by working the Witherrows) and the two trails finally separate: **Castellan bought the trinkets; Florian commissioned the body.** This is the cleanest path to the truth that *doesn't* require breaking into the conservatory — and the one most parties only reach *after* the loss.

### Sergeant Halloran — the Watch *(Human)*
*Want:* the killing to stop and the district to calm down before the festival ends in a riot — ideally by tomorrow. Tired, decent, badly out of his depth and short-staffed.
*Manner:* relieved the party came; defensive about his lack of progress; **already half-decided Dama Vesh is the one** because the district wants it and he has nothing else.
*Levers (Charisma):* persuade him to **honor the writ / deputize the party** (access to body, notes, cells), to **hold off on arresting Dama Vesh** ("give me till Midsummer"), and — late — to **back the party's accusation of a beloved man**, which he will *not* do without hard proof (the Daughter-Rose). He likes Florian too. *Everyone* likes Florian.

### Brother Anselm — the temple *(Protector Aasimar)*
Keeps the records. Kindly, overworked by the Bloomfeast rites, will help freely (no roll needed for basic access — he's glad someone's investigating). Holds the **death-record of Mira Sael**, dead of summer-fever on Midsummer "eight years gone, the poor mite — her father took it so hard the whole district learned not to mention her." Drop the Midsummer date; let the party connect it to *three nights from now.* *(His own secret: he knows Mira's grave is empty and has buried the shame of it — lean on him and the grave-robbery trail opens. See **Sexton Crale.**)*

### The wider web — more faces in the Verdance *(all innocent of murder)*

A bigger cast makes the misdirection thicker and gives the party more threads to pull, bribe, tail, and burgle. **Every one of these has a real secret and a real connection — and none of them is the killer.** Use them to fill nights, cross-light the suspects, and make the district feel lived-in.

**Doral — Florian's apprentice perfumer *(Tabaxi)*.** Eager, likable, devoted to his master. *Shady:* he's been quietly **blending cheap knockoffs of Florian's attar and pocketing the coin**, and he has a key to the shop (not the conservatory). *Connection:* he's *inside* the murder house — he's noticed Florian's pre-dawn absences and the always-locked cellar, but says nothing, half from loyalty and half because his own skimming won't survive scrutiny. *Role:* a near-miss witness gagged by his own guilt; earn him (or catch the skim) and he hands over Florian's nights out and the locked conservatory — pure opportunity (→ Conclusion 4).

**Madame Carow — the festival's "Briar Bride" seer *(Changeling)*.** A magnetic fraud who reads fortunes and sells warding-charms from a tent at the Bloommarket. *Shady:* she is **getting rich off the panic** — and has been *embellishing and inventing* Briar Bride "sightings" to keep fear (and sales) high. *Connection:* she's the rumor-mill's engine, and (like Florian) she *profits from the legend.* *Role:* a misdirection generator — her planted "sightings" pollute the witness pool; sorting her inventions from the real flower-cart sighting is half the investigative work. A vulture, not a killer.

**Ser Aldric Venn — the spurned suitor *(Flame Dragonborn)*.** A handsome, hot-tempered young noble who was infatuated with the actress **Lysenne** and publicly humiliated when she refused him weeks before she died. *Shady:* he **threatened her** in front of witnesses, then paid to bury the gossip — and he has no alibi for that night. *Connection:* Victim 1, and tangled with **Ysolde** (both orbit Lysenne's death). *Role:* the textbook jealous-lover suspect; a hot, narrow red herring that — like Ysolde's — dissolves the moment the party tests it against Pateros and Castan, whom he had no reason to touch.

**Sexton Crale — the temple gravedigger *(Mountain Dwarf)*.** Surly, drinks, keeps the consecrated ground. *Shady:* years ago he **took a cut to let Skein dig in the temple grounds** — including Mira's grave — and has been silent from fear and guilt ever since. *Connection:* the linchpin's inside man — he bridges **Brother Anselm**, the **empty grave**, **Skein**, and ultimately Florian's commission. *Role:* a fast route to motive: bribe or intimidate him and you skip straight to *"the flower-man paid to have a child dug up."* Innocent of the murders.

**Nessa — the unlicensed flower-girl (the buried witness) *(Fairy)*.** Young, sweet, frightened of the Watch. *Shady (barely):* she sells flowers without a license and **Deputy Roon has been shaking her down.** *Connection:* she is the human face of the clue Roon buried — she **saw the flower-cart on the high terrace before dawn** but won't come forward for fear of the Watch. *Role:* reach her through kindness, through the Witherrows, or by breaking Roon's racket, and the cart sighting comes clean (→ Conclusion 4). A victim of the system, not a suspect.

**Magistrate Vell — the party's employer *(Vedalken)*.** Polished, political, hired the party with the writ. *Shady:* he wants the case closed **fast and quietly to protect the Bloomfeast's lucrative name**, and he will lean on the party toward the *convenient* answer (Dama Vesh) and **refuse to let them publicly accuse a beloved citizen like Florian without ironclad proof.** *Connection:* the institutional pressure behind the whole adventure — he's *why* a warrant is hard and an accusation must be airtight. *Role:* a clock and a wall, not a killer; a Charisma obstacle in his own right.

## The evidence — the three-clue web

Lay the clues out by **conclusion**. The party needs each conclusion to crack the case; each has three independent sources so no miss is fatal. **Hand these out for looking, not for rolling** (rolls deepen them).

### Conclusion 1 — "The roses are the killer's own rare graft."
1. A gardener's eye or **Wilderness/Crafting (Hard 15)** on the bloom: this is no common briar — it's a **unique cultivar, a deep true vermilion, grown in only one place in the south** — the Vermilion Garden, Florian's graft ("Sael's Vermilion").
2. **Dama Vesh** (if earned): *"You think I grew that? Look close. Mine's common red, five petals, thorny as sin. That on the bodies is his graft — Sael's Vermilion — and there's not another rose like it for two hundred miles."*
3. **Florian himself** will (fatally, eventually) identify his own rose if shown one — he can't help admiring it — and then over-explains how *anyone* could have one (he gives them away at the festival). The over-explanation is itself a tell to a sharp **Insight (Mind)**.

### Conclusion 2 — "The wound is a fine craftsman's cut, and the blood was carried off."
1. **Medicine (Hard 15)** on a body, or **Dama Vesh**, or the temple's washing-women: one clean, deliberate, *practiced* cut from a very fine, slightly curved blade — not the ragged tear of a footpad's knife or a "beast's claw." **No ghost did this; a careful hand did, with a tool it knew.**
2. Examining **Grenn's** pruning-hooks (once you suspect him): too coarse and straight — *rules him out as the cutter even though he had the body.*
3. The **drained blood**: the bodies are near-bloodless but the scenes have little blood — *the blood was collected and carried away.* Nobody carries off blood for a robbery or a haunting. (A strong tell that this is a *ritual*, not a killing for hate or coin.)

### Conclusion 3 — "The victims were chosen for being *radiant*, and each spoke with Florian first."
1. The district, coaxed (Charisma): every victim was *beloved and bright* — the toast of the theater, the sweetest old man in the market, the festival's golden boy. Not enemies of anyone. *Why these three?*
2. Witnesses placed near each victim's last days recall — once pressed — **Florian chatting with them, giving them blooms, asking kind, oddly specific questions** ("that earring's lovely — your mother's, you said?"). Nobody thought twice: *everyone* talks to the flower-man.
3. The **keepsakes**: each victim lost the *one thing they loved most* — the mother's pearl, the wife's locket, the silver crowning-pin. A robber takes coin; this killer takes *meaning.*

### Conclusion 4 — "Florian Sael. Means, opportunity, motive."
1. **Opportunity:** he walks the entire district alone before dawn gathering blooms — the only person who can be at every murder site at the murder hour without a soul questioning it. (**Grenn's** dawn sighting of the flower-cart on the high terraces, and the attar on the breeze, nails the timing.)
2. **Motive:** the temple record + Dama Vesh — **Mira Sael died on Midsummer; the anniversary is in three nights; Florian "never buried his grief, just kept growing roses where she's planted."**
3. **Proof:** the **conservatory Daughter-Rose** (Scene 5) — a girl-shaped briar veined with blood and woven with *the victims' three keepsakes.* This is the smoking gun; everything else is enough to get a warrant or a confession, this is enough to hang him.

> **The minimum to act:** any party that has Conclusion 1 (his rose) **and** either the conservatory or the Mira-motive can force the endgame. A Charisma-heavy party that has the *pattern* and *motive* can skip the break-in entirely and **bluff a confession** (Scene 6, social route). Don't require all four — require *enough to be sure*, and let them decide when that is.

## Ways in — talk, lie, steal, bribe, break in

Investigation isn't just asking questions. **Reward every angle**, and layer misdirection into each so that a method which *works* can still point the wrong way. Most of these are **night** plays; each favors a different skill, so the whole party gets the spotlight, not just the face.

**Talk & charm (day) — Charisma.** The open road, covered in Scenes 2–3.

**Bribery — gold + Charisma.** Coin moves the Verdance.
- Bribe **Deputy Roon** for the Watch case file, morgue access, or a blind eye to move after dark. *Misdirection:* his file is curated to hide his racket — it omits the buried flower-girl statement and quietly steers toward Dama Vesh.
- Bribe stagehands at **Castellan's** theater for gossip and the layout of his locked gallery. *Misdirection:* they're certain "the Maestro did it — you should *see* what he keeps down there."
- Bribe a **Witherrows** local to point you at **Skein** the resurrection-man.

**Lying, deception & impersonation — Charisma (Deception).**
- Pose as festival inspectors, perfume-buyers, or temple census-takers to walk uninvited into homes, shops, and the theater.
- **Bluff into Florian's conservatory** — *"we hear your grafts are the secret to the rose; show us?"* — a real route to the smoking gun (Scene 5).
- Flash an over-stated **warrant** to pressure Halloran or search a suspect. *Misdirection:* a great lie gets you *in*; what you find still has to be read right — Castellan's gallery is full of stolen *grave-goods*, damning and innocent of murder.

**Theft & sleight of hand — Agility (Sleight of Hand / Stealth).**
- Steal **Castellan's ledger** — it records his grave-goods buys, *including Mira Sael's*, the thread to Skein and the true motive (read past Castellan, not stopping at him).
- Lift a **key** to a locked room, pocket a **love-letter** from Castan's effects, or palm a **rose sample** from a scene before the Watch logs it.
- Steal **Ysolde's** account-book — the bliss-attar dealing and her motive against Lysenne, a fat red herring.
- *Misdirection:* almost every document you lift implicates its owner of *something* — just rarely the murders.

**Breaking & entering (night) — Stealth / Sleight of Hand / Athletics.** The big night plays. Each has a **find**, a **misdirection**, and a **risk** (caught by the Watch → mob clock + writ trouble; caught by the killer → climax early; and remember *you are out in the dark on a killing night*):
- **Sael's Perfumery → the locked conservatory.** The truth: the Daughter-Rose (Scene 5). The hardest, best-guarded, and most damning prize.
- **Castellan's gallery.** A morbid hoard of funerary art and relics — a locket twin to a victim's, grave-goods stamped with the temple seal. *Screams* killer. Is a fence's stash. The great misdirection.
- **The temple ossuary / records vault.** **Mira Sael's grave is empty** — her remains taken years ago. A motive bombshell — *and* it reads as ordinary grave-robbing (→ Castellan), and Brother Anselm has been hiding the theft out of shame.
- **The Watch-house evidence locker.** Roon's **buried flower-girl statement** ("a flower-cart on the high terrace before dawn") — a clean Conclusion-4 clue, in a drawer because a corrupt cop didn't want it found.
- **Dama Vesh's cottage.** The bloody shears and red roses that look so damning — and, read properly, *exonerate* her (common stock, not the graft).

**Tailing & stakeout (night) — Stealth + Insight.**
- **Tail Castellan:** to a black-market relic auction or a decadent party. A dead end for murder — and a vivid scene that *feels* like a lead.
- **Tail Florian:** he walks the dark gardens with basket and knife, gathering blooms — *and on Night 2, hunting.* Catch him in the act and you can crack it early (the fast 30%). But he's careful: a botched tail (failed Stealth) means he *makes* you, changes his plans, and someone he'd otherwise have spared dies in your place.
- **Stake out the gardens:** the party may interrupt or witness the Night-2 killing — the surest way to prevent the loss, and the riskiest way to spend a night.

> **Layer the misdirection (the core trick).** The *most incriminating* finds — Castellan's relic hoard, the robbed grave, Ysolde's motive, Grenn's stolen purse — are all real, all damning, and all **innocent of murder.** The *true* clues — the unique rose, the fine curved cut, the dawn flower-cart, the *commissioned* body — are quieter, and easy to skip past while chasing the loud ones. **That gap is exactly why most parties lose someone.**

## Scene 1 — Arrival, and the foot of the Vermilion Garden

> *Read aloud:*
>
> *The Verdance hits you like a wave — heat and music and ten thousand flowers, garlands strung over every door, wine running in the gutters, the whole district drunk on midsummer. And above it all, on the high hill, the Vermilion Garden: terrace on terrace of roses so red they look like the hill is bleeding beautifully into the sun. But at its foot, where the festival crowd won't go, a stretch of garden is roped off, and a Watch-sergeant stands over a patch of trampled blooms with the look of a man who hasn't slept. He sees your writ and exhales like he's been holding his breath for a week.*

The party arrives in the late golden afternoon, three days after the third killing. **Sergeant Halloran** meets them, reads the magistrate's writ with naked relief, and — for the price of a little Charisma (or just plain decency) — gives them the run of the district: the scene, his notes, the bodies in the temple's cold-vault, and the cells if they make an arrest.

**At the scene (no rolls to find):**
- The roped-off patch at the garden's foot where **Castan**, the Garland-Prince, was found three nights ago.
- A single **perfect vermilion rose**, bagged by the Watch, "laid right on his chest. Same as the others — a flower for a keepsake. Folk are saying it's the Briar Bride's token."
- The faint, gorgeous ghost of a **perfume** still clinging to the bagged rose and the notes — sweet, complex, unplaceable.
- From the high terrace above, the party can see the whole district — **all three murder sites are visible from the Vermilion Garden.** (Tactics/Investigation, or just looking: *whoever tends this hill can watch the whole hunting-ground.*)

**Halloran gives them, if asked:** the three names, the three sites, the pattern of the roses and the missing keepsakes and the perfume, the "Briar Bride" theory, and — reluctantly — that **he's under pressure to arrest Dama Vesh, "the Witherrows poison-woman,"** because "she's got the red roses, she's got the bloody shears, she was *seen* over Pateros's body, and the district won't sleep till someone's behind a door." He doesn't love it. He has nothing else.

**And then Florian arrives.** Have the perfumer come up the terrace to deadhead the roses for the evening — *warm, sorrowful, helpful* — to introduce him early as a friend. He grieves Castan ("I crowned that boy with my own hands not a month ago — and Lysenne sang in my shop while I worked, gods, the *quiet* now"), presses free vials of his festival attar on the party, offers any help they need, and asks — gently, kindly — *"do you have any idea who could do such a thing? to them, of all people?"* **Plant him as an ally. The reveal lands harder the more they liked him.**

## Scene 2 — The district talks (investigation)

Open and non-linear. The party fans out (splitting is *safe and good* here — [../../rules/16-co-gm-guide.md](../../rules/16-co-gm-guide.md)). The Verdance is **frightened beneath the festival cheer, and wary of outsiders poking at its big week**; this is where Charisma earns its keep. For each source, set a Charisma check with the tiered table — *or skip the roll and just give it to a player who roleplays a genuinely good approach.*

- **The Bloommarket & the Gilded Finch.** The best places to *hear the district.* Coax the crowd (Persuasion/Performance) and you get: the victims were all beloved and bright; the Briar Bride legend in full; the rising fury at Dama Vesh; and the detail that **Lysenne and Castan had both been "always in Sael's shop, the darlings"** the weeks they died. (→ Conclusion 3.) A bad roll here (or a heavy-handed one) *spreads fear* and **advances the mob clock.**
- **The Tea-Gardens and the garden-foot.** The other two scenes. Old blood among the flowers. Lysenne's theater company will talk (Charisma) — she was loved, had a secret sweetheart, treasured her mother's pearl earring. The garden-folk point at **Grenn** ("found the poor prince, didn't he? Always him first") and grumble about **Dama Vesh** ("we saw her *over* old Pateros").
- **The Temple of the Summer Mother (Brother Anselm).** Free help. The three bodies lie in the cold-vault — **Medicine (Hard 15)** reads the fine clean cut and the drained blood (Conclusion 2). The records hold **Mira Sael's Midsummer death** (Conclusion 4) — Anselm offers it freely if anyone asks about old griefs or the *real* Briar Bride.
- **The Witherrows.** Shunned, watchful, untouched by the festival. Getting anyone here to talk takes the *right* Charisma (warmth and respect, not authority or coin) — but they know the most, because they know **Dama Vesh** is no killer and they're terrified the district will come for her. The road to Dama Vesh runs through earning their trust.
- **Sael's Perfumery.** He welcomes them warmly, shows off his beautiful work and his roses (the bloom on the bodies is *his* graft — a sharp eye catches it here), talks about the victims with real grief, and **keeps the conservatory locked** ("just the rare grafts — they can't bear strangers, you understand"). Every visit, he's helpful and asks his tender, too-specific questions. The more they like him, the better.

> **Co-GM note:** track who the party talks to and what they've established (Conclusions 1–4). Surface what they're *missing* — if they've never questioned the wound, have a washing-woman mention it unprompted; if they've ignored the rose, have a flower-girl coo over "the pretty one the Bride left." The three-clue web means you can always feed the missing thread through a *different* mouth.

## Scene 3 — The suspects (interrogation & misdirection)

The heart of the mystery: **three suspects, two innocent, one beloved.** Run these as real social scenes. Charisma *presses*; Insight (Mind) *reads*; the truth is fixed (Running a Mystery, rule 3).

**Dama Vesh** — the obvious answer, and wrong. She's hostile to anyone who comes at her as "the witch." A party that *threatens* her gets stonewalled and **feeds the mob** (she complains loudly, the district hears the law suspects her too). A party that earns her — humility, respect, a Persuasion that treats her as a person — gets the **rose distinction (→ Conclusion 1)**, the **wound read (→ Conclusion 2)**, and **Mira Sael's story (→ Conclusion 4)**. She is the single richest source in the game *and* the one the dice-and-charm system most rewards approaching well. Make the lesson land: **the scary one is the key witness, and only a good heart gets her.**

**Grenn** — the creep with means. Shifty, lies about his dawn movements, sweats. **Intimidation** cracks him; **Persuasion** that "we're hunting a murderer, not a purse-snatch" opens him. Either way he breaks down and confesses to **robbing Castan's corpse** — not killing — and gives up the **flower-cart on the high terraces and the attar on the breeze, at the dead hour before dawn** (→ Conclusion 4, opportunity). If the party never works him right, he stays a tempting false lead — and if they *arrest* him, the real killer kills again (Clock A).

**Florian** — interview him as a *witness and ally*, and he's flawless: helpful, grieving, full of useful (mis)direction (he'll gently steer them toward Dama Vesh, or "some madman in town for the festival"). The cracks only show to a party already carrying the threads:
- Show him a rose → he admires his own graft before catching himself, then *over-explains* who he gives them to (Insight/Mind, Hard: the over-explanation is a tell).
- Name **Mira** → he goes still, kind, and changes the subject. A gentle Persuasion ("I'm sorry — I heard you lost your daughter") gets sorrow; a *pressing* one gets the first flicker of something colder.
- Ask to see the conservatory → warm refusal ("the grafts, truly, they're so delicate"). Refusing is itself data.

**The decoys** — most parties will spend the middle of the session here, and *should.* Run them as full, satisfying suspects:
- **Castellan Vane** all but *performs* guilt — he's thrilled to be suspected, drops macabre hints, and the deeper the party digs (his gallery, his ledger, his ties to the dead) the guiltier he looks. A party that arrests or corners him on **Day 2 / Night 2** has done good detective work and reached the *wrong* answer; let them feel triumphant, and let Clock A bite that night (Scene 5b). His real crime — fencing Mira's grave-goods — is the thread that, pulled *past* him to Skein, eventually points at Florian.
- **Madame Ysolde** cracks (Charisma/Insight) into a confession of *dealing and blackmail-buying*, not murder — and only connects to Lysenne, never the others. A red herring that dissolves under cross-examination of the *other* two victims.
- **Deputy Roon** is the party's "helpful" source whose help keeps steering wrong; catching him in his racket (or in the buried statement) flips him from misdirection into a Conclusion-4 clue.

**The gentle mirrors — when the party reasons toward "the killer's a kind soul with a dead loved one and a locked room," let that logic land them on the *wrong* gentle soul first:** **Edran** the beekeeper (out in the gardens at every dawn, just like the killer, curved blade and all) or **Lensa** the dollmaker (recreating the dead in a locked studio, holding likenesses of all three victims). Both are innocent and heartbreaking to suspect — and both *feel* exactly as guilty as Florian does on vibe alone. They're the proof that this case can only be cracked on **evidence**, never on "who seems nice." (See **The cast → The gentle ones.**)

**Do not let the party get railroaded onto Dama Vesh.** If they're convinced it's her, that's *fine and intended* — let them act on it, and Clock A bites: Midsummer comes, or a fourth body drops while their "killer" sits in the cells, and they realize their error with blood on their hands. Failing forward, not failing dead.

## Scene 4 — The mob rises (the Charisma set-piece)

When the mob clock hits step 3 — triggered by the calendar (it *will* reach this by night two or three) or by a public fumble — the district comes for Dama Vesh. This is the adventure's signature **social combat**, resolved with **no monsters and no HP**, entirely on Charisma.

> *Read aloud:*
>
> *They come down into the Witherrows with festival torches and a borrowed courage that smells like spilled wine — thirty, forty of them, faces you've seen laughing in the market this very afternoon, the tavern-keeper and a theater-man and half the Bloommarket at their head, garlands still in their hair. "The witch! The Briar Bride! She'll not take another!" Dama Vesh's gate is barred. Her garden wall is already daubed with paint. And the sergeant is somewhere behind you, doing nothing, because these are his neighbors and his Watch is forty men against a hundred thousand drunk on midsummer.*

Run it as a **skill challenge** ([../../rules/13-encounters-and-rewards.md](../../rules/13-encounters-and-rewards.md)): the party needs **3 successes before 2 failures**, escalating targets, **all Charisma** (with creative use of other tools welcomed). Examples:
- **Persuasion (Hard 15):** name the holes in the case against Dama Vesh — *the rare graft she doesn't grow, the fine cut, the carried-off blood.* (If they've done the legwork, give **advantage**; if they're bluffing with nothing, **disadvantage** — the crowd smells empty words.)
- **Intimidation (Hard 15):** stand in the gate with the Watch writ and the threat of the law. (Risk: a *failure* here turns the mob *on the party.*)
- **Performance / a rousing speech (Hard 15):** redirect their fear — *"you want the killer? help us find the real one, tonight"* — and turn a lynch-mob into a search party.
- **A name to give them:** if the party can credibly point the crowd somewhere *true* (or at least away from Dama Vesh), that's an automatic success — but pointing them at the wrong person (Grenn) just moves the lynching, and *that* death is on the party too.

**Outcomes:**
- **Party wins the challenge:** the crowd disperses (or becomes searchers). Dama Vesh lives, and — grudgingly grateful — becomes a willing source if she wasn't already. Mob clock resets to step 1.
- **Party fails:** the mob takes Dama Vesh. **She dies** (or is badly hurt and flees, GM's mercy). The district believes the case closed; the sergeant won't act on Florian now; and **Florian is free to bloom on Midsummer.** A grim, fully-earned bad turn — but *still not a dead end* (the conservatory and the Midsummer climax remain; the party just lost their best witness and the district's trust).

Non-Charisma parties *can* clear this — barricade the gate (Athletics), a show of force, a cast *Fear* of their own — but the **intended, rewarded, cleanest** path is talking forty frightened people down off a murder. Let the Charisma PC own this scene.

## Scene 5 — The perfumery and the conservatory

Sooner or later the party wants *into Florian's locked conservatory.* Three honest routes — **reward whichever they invent**, and note the first is the Charisma route:

- **Get invited in (Charisma).** Befriend or bluff Florian into a tour — *"show us your craft, we're trying to trace the rose"* — or catch him out and *persuade* him the only way to clear his name is to open the door. (He'll resist hard; this needs real leverage or a great roll. If they pull it off, he's improvising a lie the whole way in — and the Daughter-Rose is *right there.*)
- **A warrant (Charisma → the law).** Persuade Halloran there's enough to search a beloved man's home. He needs Conclusion 1 *plus* one of 2/3/4 before he'll cross Florian. Then he comes with them — useful, because a sergeant seeing the Daughter-Rose is what makes the accusation *stick.*
- **Break in (Stealth/Sleight of Hand/Athletics).** While Florian's out on his pre-dawn bloom-gathering (i.e. *in the dark hours, when he may be hunting* — tension). The conservatory door is locked (Sleight of Hand, Hard) or stout (Athletics, Hard). Getting caught by a returning Florian tips straight into Scene 6.

### The conservatory (combat — Threat 6)

The fragrant shop gives onto a humid stair, and the air gets thick, hot, and *sweet* — overpoweringly sweet.

> *Read aloud:*
>
> *The conservatory is a held breath of summer — sweltering, dripping, the air so heavy with perfume it slurs your thoughts. And it is full of roses. They climb every wall, vermilion and enormous, and at the center, rooted in a great urn of dark earth, is a briar grown into the shape of a girl: life-size, eight years old, her face half-formed in rose and thorn, her hollow stems veined with something dark you understand, after a moment, is blood. Woven into her branches like jewels are a pearl earring, a worn locket, a silver garland-pin. Around her, fresh stems wait, empty, for one more. And as your shadows fall across her, the briars on the walls begin, with a sound like tearing silk, to move.*

The Daughter-Rose is the **smoking gun** (Conclusion 4, proof) — the three keepsakes, the blood, the empty stems waiting for a fourth. **Anyone who sees it knows.**

The thick perfume is a **hazard**: on entering, and at the start of each round, a creature that breathes it rolls **Spirit or Mind (Easy 10)** or has **disadvantage** on its next attack as its head swims (a held breath, a cast *Cleanse*, or simply backing to the stair avoids it — reward the clever).

Florian keeps the conservatory guarded. The instant the party is inside (whether they broke in or he brought them), his briars wake:
- **2 × Thornling** (Threat 3 each) — animated rose-brambles that tear off the walls.

This is a short, sharp fight (~40% budget) — a punctuation mark, not a crucible. Its real job is to **trap the party in the room with the proof** and to **bring Florian** (if he wasn't already here): the noise calls him home from his rounds, and the climax (Scene 6) begins on the conservatory stair. *(If the party got here by bluffing Florian into a tour, skip the Thornlings — he's standing right there, and the moment the party's eyes land on the Daughter-Rose, go straight to Scene 6.)*

## Scene 5b — If the party stalls: the fourth murder

If a night ends with the party lost, fixated on Dama Vesh/Grenn, or — most commonly — having **just cornered or jailed a decoy** (Castellan, Ysolde) and thinking the case closed (**Clock A**), Florian kills again. This is the **Night-2 pivot** the whole adventure leans toward (see **The intended arc**). **Make it someone they met and liked** — the tavern-keeper's daughter who poured their wine, the flower-girl who showed off "the pretty one the Bride left," a witness who trusted them.

Run the new scene like Scene 1, but stack it to do three jobs at once:
1. **Clear the decoy.** The murder happens while Castellan sits in a cell (or while Ysolde was demonstrably elsewhere) — *whoever the party just accused could not have done this.* Their case collapses in one stroke. Make them feel it.
2. **Hand them the clue they missed.** The killer is **bolder and sloppier now**: drop in whichever Conclusion the party still lacks — a witness who *this time* saw the flower-cart, a trail of fallen petals leading toward the perfumery, a vermilion rose still dewed and warm from his hands, or Skein turning up murdered for what he knew.
3. **Make it personal.** A name and a face, not a statistic.

This is the **cost of being wrong made personal** — not a punishment that ends the game, but a death that clears the board and points them, finally, the right way. Use it **once.** If they're still lost after, route them hard: Dama Vesh or the sergeant says aloud what everyone's missed — *"the only soul out every dawn among those flowers, and not one of us ever thought to wonder why."*

## Scene 6 — Midsummer (the climax)

However they get here — bluffed into the conservatory, caught breaking in, or confronting him in his shop with the evidence — the confrontation with Florian is the climax. **It has a social spine and a combat spine, and a Charisma party can win it almost entirely on the social one.**

> *Read aloud (he is found at the Daughter-Rose, or comes down the stair behind them):*
>
> *He doesn't run. He doesn't even look afraid — he looks at the girl of rose and thorn with her pearl and her locket and her little silver pin, and his eyes are wet, and when he turns to you his voice is so gentle it's worse than a scream.*
>
> *"You weren't supposed to see her yet. She's not finished. One more — just one more warmth and she'd open her eyes — " he stops, cups a half-formed cheek of petals. "You think I'm a monster. I'm not. The fever took her at eight years old and the world just... went on, wasting all its warmth on people who throw it away. Lysenne was going to drink her gift to death. Pateros was dying anyway. Castan — Castan glowed so bright, and he'd have let it gutter out in some marriage to a merchant's daughter. I only gathered what they'd have squandered. I made it bloom. Tonight she wakes. Please. Please don't make me hurt you. Just — let me finish. It's almost kind."*

He believes every word. **Play him sorrowful, certain, and reasonable** — the horror is that he sounds *sane.*

### The two ways to end it

**The social spine (the Charisma win).** The party can end this *without* a fight:
- **Get a confession in front of a witness.** If the sergeant or any district-folk are present (the warrant route, or the party brought witnesses), pressing Florian into saying what he's said — on the record — *ends it.* He's so sure he's righteous he'll **confess proudly** to anyone who'll grant him the dignity of understanding (Persuasion, Hard — *advantage* if they engage his belief instead of condemning it). That's the arrest. That's the clean win.
- **Talk him down.** Convince him Mira is *gone* — that this briar is a grave dressed as a homecoming; that the warmth he's stealing is the very thing he's killing; that the *real* Mira would be horrified by what's bloomed in her name. A genuinely hard Charisma scene (Persuasion vs. his Charisma defense, **Hard 15, then Heroic 20** for the real break) — but if the party lands it, he **tears the Daughter-Rose apart with his own hands**, sinks down among the petals, and weeps, and waits for the rope. The best possible ending, and only a Charisma party gets it.

**The combat spine.** If he can't be talked down — or the party doesn't try, or it's already Midsummer and the ritual is racing — he fights. Scale by timing:

- **Caught *before* Midsummer (the common case): Threat 16 (~107%).**
  - **Florian Sael** (Threat 10) + **2 × Thornling** (Threat 3 each). A tense but winnable fight against a slippery caster and his briars.
- **Midsummer night, ritual completing (the slow party): ~Threat 26 (~170%).**
  - As above, **plus the Bloomwake** (Threat 10) — the Daughter-Rose opens her eyes mid-fight (round 2–3) into a thorned, grieving construct boss. This is a *real* danger and a PC can die. It is the price of three squandered days.

### The win-condition object — the Daughter-Rose

Like the Vesper Bell and the Great Lantern before it, this fight has a **spine object**: the **girl-shaped briar.** Seed this through the clues so the party *earns* the knowledge ([../../rules/16-co-gm-guide.md](../../rules/16-co-gm-guide.md) — "seed the win button in the fiction"):

- **While the Daughter-Rose stands**, Florian will not flee, fights with **fanatic resolve** (advantage on his first attack each round; he interposes to protect it — *Sentinel* on its square), and — on Midsummer — it is *becoming* the Bloomwake.
- **Destroy the Daughter-Rose** (it's only briar and earth — fire takes it fast, or an action: Athletics to tear it up / Arcana to unmake it, Easy 10, or any solid hit, or a thrown torch): the keepsakes drop ringing among the petals, the blood-veins wither, and **Florian breaks.** He drops to whatever HP he's at, loses his fanatic resolve, and (if risen) the Bloomwake **collapses to dead brambles.** He falls to his knees among the petals and the keepsakes and the fight is over — he surrenders, or fights on hopelessly, GM's call by how it's played.
- **Mechanically:** destroying it is the clean way to short-circuit the whole climax. A clever party will realize the *briar*, not the man, is the heart of the room — and that **fire** ends it fastest. **Reward it** — it's the thematic kill-shot and it spares them the Bloomwake.

### Tactics

- **Florian** opens with **Intoxicating Bloom** (charm) on the most dangerous-looking PC, trying to turn a blade on their friends rather than him — *"you don't want to hurt me, none of you do."* Then he keeps to the thickest roses, using **Drift of Petals** to stay obscured and near the Daughter-Rose, away from melee.
- He **calls a Thornling** each round from the walls (his replenishing wave / difficulty dial).
- **The Bloomwake** (if risen) goes for the strongest PC with **Thorn-Embrace** (grapple + rend) and uses **Keening** to Frighten — *anti-fear tools and **fire** are the answers* (she's living briar; she burns).
- **When the Daughter-Rose is destroyed**, end it. Don't grind a broken man to 0 HP unless the players insist — the *point* was reaching him.

## Resolution

**Florian caught (confessed, talked down, or beaten) before Midsummer:**

> *Read aloud:*
>
> *They lay the keepsakes out on the sergeant's table — a pearl earring, a worn locket, a silver garland-pin — and the families come, one at a time, to take their dead back from the man the whole district loved. Dama Vesh stands at the edge of the Bloommarket, unburned, and watches them not-look at her, and says nothing, which from Dama Vesh is almost forgiveness. The festival stumbles on, a little quieter. And Florian Sael goes to the magistrate in irons, weeping for a girl eight summers in the ground, certain to the last that he was only ever being kind.*

**How clean it is depends on the clocks:**
- **Cracked it fast, mob defused, no fourth body:** a clean save. Three dead, the killer caught, the innocent unharmed, the Verdance whole. The best ending.
- **Fourth body / slow:** bittersweet — an extra grave, a name that didn't have to be on the stone. Make it a person they knew.
- **Mob killed Dama Vesh:** the killer's caught, but the district has its *own* murder now, and the party carries that they let it happen. A grim, true cost.
- **Wrongly arrested Grenn/Dama Vesh and only caught Florian late:** they got there, but blood was spilled they could have saved. Honor it.

**Midsummer reached / Bloomwake fought:** if they beat the risen briar, the win is harder-won and the conservatory is ash and torn petals; if a PC fell, the Verdance buries a hero beside its murdered, and Florian — if alive — is unmade by the sight of his Mira burned.

### Rewards

- **400 gold** total from the magistrate (200 advance + 200 on a confession or a body), plus the **grateful gifts of the Verdance** if the district came through whole (free festival-rights, a standing welcome, a rose named after the party in the Vermilion Garden).
- Each PC gains **1 level** (milestone to **level 4**) — unlocking their next component perk ([../../rules/03-progression.md](../../rules/03-progression.md)).
- **One magic item**, GM's choice — recommended: the **Truth-Attar**, a tiny vial of Florian's flawless make recovered from the shop — *once per rest, breathe it in as an action to know with certainty whether the next thing a creature says to you is a lie* (an Insight aid, fittingly *truth* drawn from the work of a great liar). Or, for a Charisma party, **Lysenne's Pearl**, recovered from the briar: *once per rest, wear it and sing/speak as an action to grant yourself advantage on your next Charisma roll, and clear Frightened from yourself and allies in Near range.*
- **Information / campaign hooks:**
  - **Mira Sael's grave was found empty years ago** and the temple hushed it up — *whatever Florian planted in that urn, where did the rest of her go?*
  - Florian's vermilion roses are **all over the Verdance** — he's given them away for years. Some were laid on graves the district wrote off as fevers and accidents. **How many Midsummers has he worked?**
  - In the magistrate's cells, Florian asks — gently, certainly — to speak with the party again. *"There's so much warmth in you. I only want to show you what I grew."*

### Failure modes
- **The party never solves it:** Midsummer comes, the ritual completes, the Bloomwake wakes and Florian vanishes into the festival crowd to begin again in another city. A campaign of a killer who got away — and a district that thinks a witch did it.
- **TPK at the climax:** Florian doesn't kill them. He's a gardener, not a butcher of the brave — he keeps their *warmth* for Mira, and the next party sent to the Verdance finds the investigators' keepsakes woven into a girl of rose and thorn who is very nearly finished now.

## Bestiary

Custom stat blocks for this adventure, built to the Tier-1 targets in [../../rules/12-monsters.md](../../rules/12-monsters.md). **The briar burns: living-rose creatures take +1d6 from fire.** Telegraph it.

> **Thornling** — Tier 1 · Threat 3
> **HP** 18 · **Armor** 14 · **Mind** 6 · **Spirit** 8 · **Charisma** 5 · **Speed** 6 (climbs walls and trellises)
> *Attack:* **Thorn-lash** `+5 vs. Armor → 1d6 + 2`. On a hit, barbs catch — the target's square sprouts grasping briar, **difficult terrain** until cleared.
> *Special:* **Burst of thorns** — when it drops to 0, it explodes into needles; each creature adjacent takes `1d4` piercing (Agility vs. `+4` for none). Immune to Charmed / Frightened / Poisoned. Takes **+1d6 from fire.**
> *Notes:* A writhing tangle of animated rose-cane and root, torn off the conservatory walls to guard the Daughter-Rose. Mindless, fast, fragile, and very flammable.

> **The Bloomwake** — Tier 1 · elite/boss · Threat 10  *(only if the ritual completes on Midsummer)*
> **HP** 70 · **Armor** 15 · **Mind** 10 · **Spirit** 13 · **Charisma** 14 · **Speed** 6
> *Attack:* **Thorn-embrace** `+6 vs. Armor → 2d8` piercing. On a hit, the target rolls **Might vs. `+6`** or is **Restrained** in coiling briar (break free: Might, Hard).
> *Attack:* **Keening** `+6 vs. Charisma → ` target is **Frightened** until the end of its next turn, Range Near — a child's voice, calling for a father, wrong.
> *Special:* **Briar Skin** — resistant to piercing and slashing (halve that damage — blades just prune her). Immune to Charmed / Frightened / Poisoned. **Burns:** takes **+1d6 from fire**, and any **fire hit** withers one Restrain she's maintaining and staggers her (no action her next turn).
> *Special:* **Woven of the Taken** — the three keepsakes are set in her chest. An adjacent PC can **tear one free** (action; Athletics or Arcana, Hard 15); each one freed drops her max HP by 20 and dims her bloom — *the warmth leaving.* Three freed, or the briar burned, and she falls to dead brambles.
> *Special:* **Rooted to the Daughter-Rose** — she *is* the briar woken. **If the Daughter-Rose is destroyed (see Scene 6), she collapses instantly.** This is the win button.
> *Notes:* Beautiful and wrong, a girl of crimson roses weeping petals, calling a father's name in a voice eight years dead. Not Mira. Never was.

> **Florian Sael** *(the perfumer)* — Tier 1 · boss · Threat 10
> **HP** 55 · **Armor** 13 · **Mind** 13 · **Spirit** 13 · **Charisma** 16 · **Speed** 6
> *Attack:* **Grafting-knife** `+5 vs. Armor → 1d6 + 3`, a fine curved blade. (The murder weapon.)
> *Attack:* **Thorn-burst** `+6 vs. Armor → 2d6` piercing, Range Near — conjured briar from any rose or planter nearby.
> *Attack:* **Intoxicating Bloom** `+6 vs. Charisma →` target is **Charmed** until the end of its next turn (it cannot attack him and sees him as a friend to protect); Range Near — a wave of drugging perfume. *"You don't want to hurt me."*
> *Special:* **Drift of Petals** — once per round as a free action, he raises a swirl of petals; attacks against him have **disadvantage** until he next attacks (the conservatory is thick with cover to vanish into).
> *Special:* **Coax the Briar** — once per round as a free action, raises **1 Thornling** from the walls (within Near). Cap at 3 on the board; dial up if the fight's easy, down ("too grieved") if it's brutal.
> *Special:* **Fanatic Resolve** — while the **Daughter-Rose stands**, he has **advantage on his first attack each round** and will not flee; he is **Sentinel** on its square, guarding it. **When the Daughter-Rose is destroyed, this ends, he loses Drift of Petals, and he breaks** (Scene 6).
> *Immunities:* none — *he is only a man.* No special damage weakness; he dies like anyone.
> *Notes:* Gentle, sorrowful, utterly certain he is being kind. Opens by charming the strongest PC, keeps to the roses, guards the Daughter-Rose with his life. Will surrender or weep when it's destroyed or his belief is broken — **he wants to be understood far more than he wants to win.**

## GM cheat sheet

Print or pin this.

**The case in one line:** the beloved perfumer & garden-keeper **Florian Sael** is draining the Verdance's brightest, most-loved people to feed a girl-shaped briar — the **Daughter-Rose** — grown over his dead daughter **Mira**, to make her bloom on **Midsummer (3 nights out)**. The district blames the innocent poison-gardener **Dama Vesh**.

**Two clocks:** *(A)* wrong accusation or no idea → **he kills again that night** (a known NPC); ritual completes on Midsummer. *(B)* fear → **mob lynches Dama Vesh** by night three unless defused (Scene 4).

**Intended arc (~70% of parties):** chase a decoy → **lose an NPC on Night 2** → that body clears the decoy and breaks the case → close in Day 3 → climax Midsummer night. Run the clocks *straight*; the loss is the spine, not a failure. Fast 30% crack the motive (empty grave → Skein → "the flower-man") or tail Florian and prevent the second death.

**Timeline:** *Days* = open game (talk, charm, bribe, view bodies, records open). *Nights* = hidden game (B&E, tailing, the mob, **the killer hunts**). Day1 scene+intros · Night1 setup/gather · Day2 wrong case builds · **Night2 the loss** · Day3 close in · **Night3 Midsummer climax.**

**Methods (all rewarded, all misdirecting):** bribe Roon (file/access — tainted) · steal Castellan's ledger (→ Skein/motive) or Ysolde's books (red herring) · deceive into the conservatory · B&E the gallery (grave-goods=decoy), the ossuary (Mira's empty grave), the Watch locker (buried cart statement), Dama's cottage (exonerates her) · tail Florian at dawn (catch him / blow it and someone dies).

**The four conclusions (3 clues each — give for looking, not rolling):**
1. **The roses are his rare graft** — Wilderness/Crafting 15 (unique "Sael's Vermilion") · Dama Vesh ("mine's common, his is that") · he admires his own graft & over-explains.
2. **Wound is a fine grafting-knife cut** (not footpad/beast), **blood carried off** — Medicine 15 · Dama Vesh · "a robber takes coin, not blood."
3. **Victims chosen for radiance; each spoke with Florian first** — district (all beloved/bright) · witnesses (Florian's tender questions, free blooms) · keepsakes taken = the thing they loved most.
4. **Florian: means/motive/proof** — only one out every dawn among the flowers (Grenn's cart-sighting) · Mira died on Midsummer (temple + Dama Vesh) · **the conservatory Daughter-Rose** (smoking gun).

**Suspects (the Verdance is full of warm people with secrets — find the killer by *evidence*, never by "who seems nice"):**
- **Florian Sael** — the killer. Beloved perfumer; flawless ally until cornered with rose + Mira + Daughter-Rose.
- **The gentle mirrors (innocent — Florian's camouflage):** **Edran** the beekeeper (in the gardens at every dawn like the killer, curved knife), **Lensa** the dollmaker (recreating the dead in a locked studio, has all 3 victims' likenesses + intimate notes), the **fever-bereaved** (many parents lost children that Midsummer). They share his every "tell" so kindness/grief/a locked room can't finger him.
- **Castellan Vane** — *decoy villain.* Theatrical death-lover; grave-goods fence who bought Mira's trinkets. Innocent of murder; the trail runs *through* him to Skein.
- **Madame Ysolde** — beloved patroness; bliss-attar dealer with a motive vs. Lysenne only. Innocent.
- **Deputy Roon** — helpful Watch officer; corrupt, buried the cart statement. Innocent; tainted source.
- **Dama Vesh** — scary recluse the mob wants; richest source if earned with *respect.* Innocent.
- **Grenn** — creepy groundskeeper; corpse-thief not killer; cracks under Intimidation, gives the cart clue. Innocent.
- **Skein** — resurrection-man; the linchpin (Florian commissioned Mira's body; Castellan bought the goods).

**Charisma is the master key:** witnesses won't talk without it; access is social; the mob is a Charisma skill challenge; the cleanest ending is a **confession or a talk-down**, not a fight. Charm *opens mouths* but never makes a false thing true.

**Climax (Scene 6):** social win = public confession (Persuasion, *advantage* if you engage his belief) **or** talk-down (Hard 15 → Heroic 20). Combat = **Threat 16** if caught early (Florian + 2 Thornlings); **~26** if the ritual completes (+ Bloomwake). **Win button: destroy the Daughter-Rose** (Easy 10 / any solid hit / fire) → Florian breaks, Bloomwake collapses. **Don't grind a broken man.**

**Monster quick reference:**

- Bloomwake — HP 70, Armor 15, `+6 → 2d8` + Restrain; Keening `+6 vs Cha` Frighten. Resist pierce/slash. Tear keepsakes (Hard) −20 HP each. Daughter-Rose destroyed → she collapses. +1d6 fire.

Fanatic Resolve + Sentinel **while Daughter-Rose stands**. Only a man — no weakness, breaks when the briar does.

**Conservatory hazard:** thick perfume — start of round, **Spirit/Mind (Easy 10)** or disadvantage on next attack (held breath / Cleanse / back to the stair avoids it).
