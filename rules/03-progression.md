# 03 — Progression

Characters grow from level 1 to level 20. By the master tier, the party are legends shaping the fate of a region or a world.

The shape of leveling is a **three-beat cycle** — *Ability → Perk → Breadth* — so every level-up hands you exactly one headline change, plus HP. You never look at a level-up and feel nothing happened, and you never get buried in choices at once.

## Two kinds of "special ability"

The game keeps these cleanly separate:

- **Signatures (2).** Your **Racial Signature** and your **Class Signature** — the unique, identity-defining moves. You get **both at level 1** and they never change (your Class Signature *ascends* to a stronger version at the level-20 capstone). See [19-classes.md](19-classes.md).
- **Perks (6).** The mechanical abilities granted by your **components** (weapon types, armor tier, schools). Each component is a **2-perk track** (a basic perk and an advanced one); you **unlock them as you level**, not all at once.

So a level-1 character has **2 signatures**. A level-20 character has **2 signatures + 6 perks + a capstone = 9 marquee abilities**, gained one at a time.

**Every level also raises HP** (`+1d6 + Might`, roll at the table; a `1` or `2` counts as `3` — no one feels punished for leveling). **Casters' mana** rises automatically too, since the pool is `level + ability`.

### The three headlines

- **Ability `+1`** (levels 2, 5, 8, 11, 14, 17 — six total). Raise one ability by `+1`, **maximum `+5`**. This is the only headline that touches your dice math, so it's rationed.
- **Perk** (levels 3, 6, 9, 12, 15, 18 — six total). **Deepen one of your components**: unlock its next perk. Basic perks come first (3/6/9), advanced perks later (12/15/18). You choose which component to advance each time.
- **Breadth** (levels 4, 7, 10, 13, 16, 19 — six total). The roster's two halves grow in parallel:
  - **Casters** learn **+1 spell** (of a tier you can currently afford). Spells known climb **3 → 9**.
  - **Everyone else** gains **+1 training** (a new skill). Trainings climb **3 → 9** (4 → 10 for Humans).
  - *(Half-casters and other gish count as casters — they take the spell.)*

## The tiers

Three tiers, the spine the whole game is balanced against — training bonus, monster difficulty, and material grade all key off them:

- **Apprentice — levels 1–7:** training bonus `+2`. Local stakes: a village, a forest, a single dungeon. PCs are dangerous to bandits and goblins; one hit from an ogre can drop them.
- **Veteran — levels 8–14:** training bonus `+3`. Regional stakes: a duchy, a frontier, a city-state. PCs are known by name and face elite enemies and lesser magic users.
- **Master — levels 15–20:** training bonus `+4`. Mythic stakes: a continent, a god, a calamity. PCs are legendary, and the monsters they fight are individually named threats.

A "Tier 1" monster is built for an Apprentice party, "Tier 2" for Veteran, "Tier 3" for Master ([12-monsters.md](12-monsters.md)). The GM picks the tier; the math takes care of itself.

## The frozen ceiling

The training bonus stops at **`+4`** and any single ability stops at **`+5`**. So the highest attack or check total in the game is **`d20 + 5 + 4 = d20 + 9`**, against the same `10 / 15 / 20` targets — **and that ceiling is reached around the veteran tier and never rises again.**

This is deliberate. The back half of the game (levels 8–20) doesn't make your numbers bigger — it makes your **toolkit** bigger: more perks, more spells, more skills, more HP. A level-20 hero isn't *more accurate* than a level-8 one; they have more answers and can take more punishment. Bounded accuracy is preserved for the whole campaign, so monster math never unravels.

## Leveling triggers

Use **milestone leveling** by default. The GM decides when the party has done enough — usually after a major scene, a completed dungeon, or a story arc closing.

A reasonable cadence:
- **Level 2** after the first real session of play.
- **Level 3** after the first proper adventure (3–5 sessions).
- From there, roughly **one level per 2–4 sessions**, faster early and slower at the master tier.

Don't track XP. Levels are punctuation in the story, not a spreadsheet. The GM may award **half-levels** for major in-between accomplishments to keep players who miss a session from falling behind.

## Resting

There is **one rest type**: roughly 8 hours of safety — sleep, a watch rotation, hot food. After a rest, each character:
- Regains **all HP**.
- Regains **all mana**.
- Recovers from any condition that doesn't have a longer duration written on it.
- Refreshes "once per rest" abilities.

There is no short rest, no hit dice, no per-encounter resource. **You are either at the fight or recovering from the fight; nothing in between.**

If the party tries to rest in unsafe terrain, the GM rolls a chance of interruption — usually `2 in 6`. If interrupted, the rest doesn't complete and resources don't refresh. **Resting between fights mid-dungeon should be hard** — it's the tension that keeps resource management interesting now that per-encounter recovery is gone.

## What progression doesn't do

- **No feats.** Growth happens via perks and signatures, not a parallel feat system.
- **No multiclassing.** A class is a commitment; reflavor instead.
- **No buying trainings off-schedule.** New trainings come on breadth levels (and, rarely, as a GM story award).
- **No magic-item attunement slots.** Items work when held or worn.
- **No XP.** Milestones only.
- **No rising accuracy past the frozen ceiling.** Levels 8–20 add tools and HP, not bigger to-hit numbers.

The numbers that change on your sheet from level 1 to 20:
- **HP** (every level).
- **Training bonus** (twice: at 8 and 15).
- **Six ability bumps**.
- **Six perks** unlocked, plus the level-20 capstone.
- **Spells known** (casters) **or trainings** (everyone else), six of them.

That's the whole growth curve. A level-20 sheet is fuller than a level-1 one, but it's still a list you can read in a minute.
